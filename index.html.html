§ III

Task Breakdown

A thirteen-day sequence ordered around a single guiding principle: all dust-generating work happens before any cleaning, and all cleaning happens before any painting. Sanding is split across two days (walls/stairs on Day Two, floor on Day Three) with overnight settling between. Painting then flows top-down: ceilings on Day Five, trim and baseboards on Day Six, walls on Days Seven & Eight, stairs on Days Eight & Nine, floor on Days Nine through Eleven. Both ceiling and trim work are optional — skip either day and renumber forward. The minimum project (no ceiling, no trim) runs eleven days. Known structural quirks — peaked joints, popped drywall nails, and the particle-board entry threshold — are handled on a move-timeframe basis: cosmetic-minimum for the drywall, full replacement only for the entry where the existing material is genuinely unfit. See the field notes below for the reasoning.

On the structural quirks — a deliberate move-timeframe call Peaked joints and popped nails are caused by ongoing seasonal framing movement, and the property has shown them since the owners acquired it. A permanent fix (re-screwing, multi-coat feathering, or injecting adhesive behind the board) is real work and tends to partially reappear within a season or two anyway, because the underlying wood movement doesn't stop. Per the owners' direction, these get the cosmetic minimum: tap proud nail heads flush and skim with spackle; a single thin skim to soften prominent peaks; subtle peaks just get painted over (a flat/eggshell sheen hides minor ridges well). The goal is simply not to lock in a visibly raised bump under fresh paint — not to chase a structural cure. The entry threshold is the one exception that does get full remediation, because particle board at a front door is genuinely unfit — it swells and crumbles with the moisture an entry sees. Replacing it with moisture-resistant plywood and painting it to match folds cleanly into the floor work already happening, so it costs little extra time while removing a real future-failure point. Before capping it, confirm with the owners there's no active water intrusion at the door — otherwise the new patch will fail the same way.
On dust discipline The reason this sequence works is that all dust-creating tasks (sanding, vacuuming) finish before any wet work begins. Sanding dust is fine enough to stay airborne for hours and settles invisibly on every horizontal surface — including walls. Painting over settled sanding dust gives you a gritty, compromised finish that's nearly impossible to fix without redoing the work. The Day 4 deep clean catches everything; nothing dust-generating happens after it. The Day 2/Day 3 split is intentional: walls and stairs on one day, then the dustiest task (floor sanding) on the next, with overnight settling between them. Cramming both into a single day works but generates more airborne dust than necessary and is harder on the body.

On ceiling painting Ceilings get painted before walls so any drips, splatter, or roller overspray ends up on still-unfinished walls (which you'll cover anyway). Reverse this order and every drip becomes a touch-up job. Use flat sheen only — any sheen on a ceiling highlights every imperfection in raking light. If any ceilings have popcorn texture, painting them risks dislodging the texture; in that case, mist the popcorn lightly with water from a spray bottle first, let it absorb for 15 minutes, then roll very gently with a 3/4" nap. Spraying is genuinely better than rolling on popcorn if you have or can borrow a sprayer.
On trim painting Trim gets painted before walls — counterintuitive, but it's how the pros work. Paint trim freely without taping the walls; any overflow gets covered when you paint the walls afterward. Then once the trim is fully cured (24 hr minimum), tape over the cured trim and paint walls cleanly against the tape. The reverse order (walls first, then trim) requires precision cutting-in along finished walls and risks lifting wall paint when you remove tape. Critical detail for this project specifically: baseboards must be painted before the floor coatings go down, not after. Otherwise you'll be cutting in along a freshly-painted floor — slow, stressful, and prone to drips.
On Krud Kutter application Apply Krud Kutter to the entire wall surface, not just visible dirty spots. Walls accumulate an invisible film of atmospheric oils, dust, and residue over time that prevents new paint from bonding properly. Diluted application (per label) for general washing; full-strength concentrate for kitchen walls, bathroom walls, areas around light switches, and anywhere furniture has been resting. Krud Kutter Original is no-rinse for paint prep — meaning you can paint directly over it once dry, with no water rinse step required.
On working yourself out For both floor coats, plan the start and exit before you begin. The farthest corner from the door is where you start; the door is where you finish. Once the floor is wet, you cannot return. Same logic applies to the stairs — paint from the top down, end at the bottom step, and stay off until cured.
§ IV

Closing Recommendations

Field-tested tips from professional painters and accumulated wisdom for getting the result to last. Pay attention to the small habits — they are the difference between a paint job that lasts five years and one that lasts five months.

01Box your paint

Paint color varies slightly from can to can, even within the same SKU. Combine all the gallons you'll use into a 5-gallon bucket and stir thoroughly before starting. Eliminates visible color shifts mid-wall.

02Maintain a wet edge

When rolling, always blend into still-wet paint. Don't let one section dry before joining it to the next — that's how lap marks form. Work corner to corner, full wall height, before stopping.

03Cut in just before rolling

Brush-applied edges have a slightly different texture than roller-applied centers. If the brush work dries before the roller catches up, you'll see a halo effect. Cut in one wall, roll it, then move to the next.

04Score tape before removing

Once paint dries on tape, removing it tears paint off the wall. After paint cures (24 hours minimum), run a sharp utility knife along the tape edge, then pull at a 45° angle slowly.

05Prime your patches

Spackle absorbs paint differently than the surrounding wall. Without spot-priming, patches show as dull spots — "flashing." A quick brush-prime over each patch eliminates the problem entirely.

06Roll then tip-off on floors

Pro technique for porch and floor enamel: roll the paint on, then immediately drag a wide brush lightly through the wet paint in the direction of the grain. Eliminates roller stipple and produces a smoother, more uniform finish.

07Damp the roller first

Before loading paint, dampen the roller sleeve with a wet rag (water for latex, mineral spirits for oil). The slightly damp nap accepts paint better and rolls more evenly on the first pass.

08Check humidity

Paint poorly in humid weather — drying slows dramatically and the finish suffers. Aim for 40–60% relative humidity and temperatures between 50–85°F. The HVAC and a hygrometer are your friends.

09Strain old paint

If using paint that's been sitting, run it through a paint strainer or fine mesh before pouring into the tray. Catches lumps and skin that would otherwise end up on your wall.

10Respect the cure time

Floor paint feels dry to the touch in hours but isn't cured for days. Light foot traffic at 72 hours; furniture at 7 days; full chemical hardness at 30 days. Skipping cure time is the #2 cause of premature floor paint failure (#1 is prep).

11Register the Ridgid

Within 90 days of purchase, register your Ridgid shop vac online for the Lifetime Service Agreement — free parts, service, and motor replacement forever. People forget; set a reminder the day you buy it.

12Music & pace

This is a 10-day project across multiple weekends. Set up a speaker, work at a sustainable pace, and don't rush — rushed paint shows. The day you're too tired to do careful work is the day to call it and resume tomorrow.

Final field note The single biggest predictor of a paint job that lasts is the prep work, not the paint. A $30 gallon of paint over careful prep will outlast a $90 gallon over rushed prep, every time. Resist the temptation to skip cleaning, scuff-sanding, or priming — those steps are not optional, they are the project. The painting itself is the easy part.
